This data revolution needs a new generation of scientists equally fluent in biology and computer technology. Our MSc programme — a collaboration between the Universities of Bern and Fribourg — trains you to become one of them.

Why Bioinformatics & Computational Biology?

Biological and medical sciences are collecting enormous amounts of data — from genome sequencing and mass spectrometry to ecological remote sensing and biological imaging. The challenge is no longer collecting data, but analysing it to unearth the knowledge hidden within. Bioinformatics is the key to understanding the mechanisms of life.

Our interdisciplinary programme teaches you to analyse large biological data sets through computer algorithms and statistical tools, and to model biological processes. Whether you come from biology, computer science, mathematics or physics — we complement your background with tailored courses and bring everyone to a common level.

Career in Industry

Life sciences, health science, food technology, pharma, biotech — bioinformatics skills are in high demand across sectors.

Academic Research

The MSc prepares you for PhD programmes in bioinformatics, computational biology, genomics, and related fields.

Broad Applications

From conservation biology and drug design to epidemiology, biomedical engineering, and personalised medicine.

Two Universities, One Degree

Benefit from the combined expertise and research infrastructure of both Bern and Fribourg, with competitive student-researcher ratios.

Admission & Application

Start every fall semester

The MSc in Bioinformatics and Computational Biology is open to graduates from a wide range of backgrounds. There is no separate bachelor programme — admission is granted directly based on your existing degree.

Direct Admission (no additional requirements)

BSc from a Swiss university with ≥90 ECTS in one of these fields:

Biology / Biochemistry
Bioinformatics
Life Sciences / Biomedicine
Computer Science / Informatics
Mathematics / Statistics
Physics

Admission with Additional Requirements

Up to 60 ECTS additional coursework may be required

Swiss BSc in a related field with ≥60 ECTS in one of the above
BSc from a recognised foreign university (evaluated individually)
BSc from a Swiss University of Applied Sciences (40–60 ECTS complement)
Other backgrounds (e.g. bioengineering, pharmacy) — individual evaluation

How to Apply

Apply via the Admissions Office of your chosen home university

Home university Bern: Apply via the Universität Bern Admissions Office
Home university Fribourg: Apply via the Université de Fribourg Admissions
Application deadlines: Check with your chosen home university. Typically April 30 for the fall semester.
Questions? Contact info.bioinformatics@unibe.ch

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Bioinformatics and Computational Biology?
Bioinformatics and computational biology sits at the intersection of biology, computer science, and statistics. It involves developing and applying computational methods to analyse large biological data sets — from DNA sequences and protein structures to entire ecosystems. If you are fascinated by biological questions and intrigued by what Big Data and computing power can do to tackle them, this field is for you.
Do I need a specific bachelor's degree to apply?
The programme welcomes graduates from diverse backgrounds. Direct admission (without additional requirements) is granted to holders of a Swiss BSc with at least 90 ECTS in biology, biochemistry, life sciences, biomedicine, informatics, bioinformatics, mathematics, statistics, physics, or computer science. Graduates from other fields (e.g. bioengineering, pharmacy) can also apply, but may need to complete additional coursework of up to 60 ECTS. International degrees are evaluated individually.
I come from biology — will I be able to follow the programming courses?
Yes! The programme is specifically designed for this. If you come from biology or biochemistry, you take Module A in your first semester, which includes a gentle introduction to programming (Python) and mathematical concepts. By the second semester, all students have a common foundation and follow the same curriculum.
I come from computer science — will I need to learn wet lab techniques?
If you come from CS, math, or physics, you take Module B, which introduces molecular biology and genetics — including guided lab practicals. This ensures you understand the biological context of the data you will be analysing. No prior biology knowledge is required.
What career opportunities does the programme open up?
The MSc prepares you for careers in the life science industry, health care, pharma/biotech, food technology, governmental and non-governmental organisations, as well as for a PhD in academia. Bioinformatics skills are applied in conservation biology, drug design, epidemiology, biomedical engineering, personalised medicine, and many other fields. The interdisciplinary nature of the programme and the networking opportunities during the master thesis considerably widen your job prospects.
Where can I do my master thesis?
You conduct your thesis (30 ECTS, 6 months full-time) in a research group at the University of Bern or Fribourg. It can also be carried out in collaboration with affiliated institutes such as the Swiss Institute of Bioinformatics (SIB), governmental institutions, or the private sector. The thesis can begin in the 3rd semester and extends over the 4th semester.
When does the programme start?
The MSc programme starts every year in the fall semester (Herbstsemester), typically mid-September. The Welcome Event takes place on the first day of the semester at the University of Fribourg (PER21, Salle 230). There is no spring semester intake.
How does the collaboration between Bern and Fribourg work?
Although the programme is a collaboration between the two universities, it is not a joint degree in the administrative sense. You enrol at one university as your home university and are subject to that university's regulations, exam rules, and study plan. The University of Bern follows the Studienplan and RSL Phil.-nat.; the University of Fribourg follows the Règlement de la Faculté des sciences et de médecine. This means exam repetition rules, compensation rules, and administrative procedures may differ depending on where you are enrolled. If in doubt, always check with your home university's study advisor.
What is BeNeFri and why do I need it?
BeNeFri is an agreement between the universities of Bern, Neuchâtel, and Fribourg that allows students to attend courses at the partner universities. Since about half the courses in this programme take place at the other university, you must register for BeNeFri at your home university every semester. Without this registration, you cannot access courses, materials, or exams at the partner university. Late registrations are not accepted — check the deadlines early!

In which language are courses taught?
All courses are taught in English. All exams, written work, and the Master thesis must also be in English. No German or French is required to complete the programme.
What are Module A and Module B?
These preparatory modules (6 ECTS each) ensure all students have a common foundation. If you come from biology/biochemistry, you take Module A (programming & math basics). If you come from math/CS/physics, you take Module B (molecular biology basics with lab work). Both are taken in the first semester only.
How do I register for courses at the other university?
First, you must be registered for BeNeFri at your home university (see above). Then register for individual courses via KSL (Bern courses) and MyUniFR (Fribourg courses). Important: You must register separately for exams in addition to course registration. Course materials are available on ILIAS (Bern). Check deadlines carefully — late registrations are not accepted at either university.
Can I repeat a failed exam?
Yes, failed exams can be repeated once, typically in the following semester. The examiner may replace a written exam with an oral exam (15–60 min). Within a module, one insufficient grade can be compensated if the overall module average is sufficient — except for Module A/B courses and the Master thesis, which cannot be compensated.
How do elective courses work?
You need at least 1.5 ECTS in elective courses. Electives should complement your background (i.e., come from a field different from your BSc). The choice must be approved by the study advisor. You can take courses from the suggested list or propose others.
Can external credits be recognized?
Yes, up to 10 ECTS of credits from other universities can be recognised upon request to the study advisor. The Master thesis cannot be completed externally.
Can I study part-time?
The programme is designed as a full-time programme over 4 semesters. However, individual arrangements may be possible — contact the study coordinator to discuss your situation.
Is there a bachelor programme in Bioinformatics at Bern or Fribourg?
No, there is no dedicated bachelor programme in bioinformatics at either university. The MSc programme accepts graduates from various bachelor backgrounds and brings them up to speed through the preparatory modules.
